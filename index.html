Projects

Censors at the HeLLM: Federal Word Bans in the Trump Administration - COMS BC3997: LLM Foundations and Ethics
- Worked alongside three other students as a semester-long project.

- Utilized pointwise mutual information (PMI), Gemma3, and BERT and clustering methods to determine any implicit bans present within a dataset of NSF grant proposals flagged by the U.S. Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, & Transportation and Senator Ted Cruz. The proposals were flagged because they allegedly contain 'DEI' terms (dataset available at the bottom of this site, and related Senate report available here). Note that our final project was submitted May 9, 2025.

- Beyond EDA of the the Senate-released dataset, I focused on using the BERT base model to create contextual embeddings for the flagged 'DEI' terms situated in their larger context within the proposal.
I added any flagged DEI terms that were not contained within the WordPiece tokenizer dictionary, and I further had to iterate through portions of each proposal in consideration of the limited frame of the tokenizer (512 tokens) to ensure that each context and instance of any flagged term can be accounted for throughout all proposals. To cluster the embeddings, I tested both k-means and HDBSCAN, though I eventually relied more on HDBSCAN due to more accurate contextualizations and groupings.

- Our results showed significant oversight in the Senate Committee's flagging of these terms, as the term "poc" never appeared in relation to "person/people of color," and only ever appears in reference to "point-of-care." Further, through clustering, we see that the term "trauma" appears in contexts that can be in reference to 'head' or 'brain' trauma, as well as in a context we believe is intended by the Senate Committee that can be viewed as 'DEI'-related (such that it is used in combination with other flagged terms, such as 'racial').

- The BERT and clustering code is uploaded on GitHub, under "final_bert.ipynb."
